# Governance Migration Plan

Artifact ID: ART-026
Iteration: baseline-v0.1
Status: draft
Change type: new
Owner module: MOD-GOV, MOD-LAW
Depends on: ART-009, ART-011, ART-024

## Purpose

Specify how an existing polity migrates its governance and legal institutions toward the architecture in ART-009 and ART-011 without rupturing institutional continuity or rights protection. The plan is written primarily for Mode B (reform-by-layers) from ART-024; Mode A (greenfield) and Mode C (post-crisis) get briefer specific guidance.

The objective is a transition that improves capture-resistance, legitimacy, observability, and competence without destabilizing the institutions performing those functions during the change.

## Pre-Migration Conditions

Before significant constitutional change, the following preconditions should be assessed:

- Independent judiciary functional.
- Rule of law minimally honored.
- Basic civil liberties operating.
- Sufficient public-administration capacity for transition.
- Coalition broad enough to sustain at least a decade of reform.
- Public-service media independent enough to support deliberation.

Where preconditions are absent, the first phase is preconditional reform: strengthen judiciary, build administrative capacity, protect press, restore civil liberties.

## Migration Sequence

Sequence reflects "protect first, redistribute second" and "observability before automation" principles from ART-024.

### Phase 0: Stabilization (Year -2 to Year 0)

Preconditions met or being built. Public-deliberation period launched. No major irreversible reforms yet.

### Phase 1: Foundational Protection (Years 0-2)

- Strengthen independent judiciary: secure tenure, appointment reform if needed, funding floor.
- Establish or strengthen Adversarial Review Body (GOV-B5).
- Strengthen Audit Office (GOV-B8): funding floor, mandate expansion.
- Strengthen Ombuds Network (GOV-B9).
- Pass robust anti-corruption package (LAW-AC, GOV-A).
- Whistleblower protection law (GOV-A6).
- Data-protection authority establishment (TAI-A2).
- Decision-log infrastructure begins (TAI-O1).
- Algorithmic registry begins (TAI-O4).
- Lobbying-transparency law passed (GOV-A3).
- Election-administration independence (if not present).
- Press freedom and public-service media protection (CUL-MI).
- Civil-liberties review of any existing emergency provisions; sunset clauses added.

This phase strengthens the polity's capacity to resist capture before more controversial reforms.

### Phase 2: Universal Floor and Commons (Years 2-5)

- Universal-floor program legislation; phased rollout.
- Commons-layer baseline for water, basic energy, basic transit, primary education, primary healthcare, basic digital connectivity.
- Health-system universal-access reform if not present.
- Mental-health-system reform (HEA-M).
- Housing-first homelessness program.
- Just-transition fund established for workers affected by upcoming sectoral reforms.

This phase secures the social floor before measures that would otherwise risk hardship for vulnerable populations.

### Phase 3: Sortition and Deliberative Institutions (Years 3-6)

- Citizens' Assembly (GOV-B1) established by statute; pilot binding role on selected issues.
- Future Generations Advocate (GOV-B7) established.
- Participatory-budgeting expansion.
- Citizens'-jury and deliberative-mini-public capacity built.

This phase introduces sortition and deliberation alongside (not replacing) existing electoral institutions; demonstrates compatibility before higher-stakes integration.

### Phase 4: Electoral and Constitutional Reform (Years 4-8)

- Electoral-system reform: move to mixed-member proportional or comparable (per GOV-S2) if not present.
- Campaign-finance reform: public match, contribution caps, real-time disclosure (GOV-A4).
- Revolving-door restrictions (GOV-A2).
- Term-limit and rotation rules per GOV-B2 through GOV-B7.
- Begin constitutional-convention process: drafting committee, deliberation period, public consultation.
- Adversarial-review of proposed constitutional changes (GOV-B5).
- Ratification through procedure consistent with current constitution (GOV-D2 in shadow form during transition).

### Phase 5: Economic Architecture (Years 5-10)

- Tax reform: wealth-tax and inheritance-tax instruments (ECO-T2); land-value tax (ECO-T3); externality taxes scaling (ECO-T4); automation tax (ECO-T5).
- Antitrust strengthening (ECO-MK1).
- Banking-structure reform (ECO-M2): separation of commercial and speculative banking; public banking option.
- Cooperative sector support (ECO-MK2).
- Care-economy reform: paid family leave, universal childcare, eldercare scale-up (VAL-017, ECO-W).

### Phase 6: Ecology and Infrastructure (Years 5-15)

- Ecological-cap legislation (ECN-B): GHG budget, water budgets, biodiversity floors.
- Land-tenure reform (ECN-L).
- Housing-system reform: mixed tenure scale-up (ECN-H).
- Energy transition (ECN-E).
- Reserve-duration buildout (VAL-004, ECN-IC).
- Climate-adaptation infrastructure (ECN-CA).

### Phase 7: Technology and AI (Years 5-10, ongoing)

- AI risk classification operating (TAI-R).
- AI safety regulator (TAI-A1) functional.
- Algorithmic-audit and impact-assessment programs.
- Public-sector digital reform: open standards, open source, in-house capacity (TAI-PS).
- Cybersecurity baseline raised for critical infrastructure (TAI-S).

### Phase 8: Defense and External (Years 5-15)

- Doctrine alignment (DEF-M1).
- Civil-defense buildout (DEF-D).
- Migration-and-asylum reform (DEF-MIG).
- Boundary-audit program (DEF-B8).
- Foreign-policy realignment toward principled multilateralism (DEF-F).

### Phase 9: Constitutional Anchoring (Years 8-12)

- Final constitutional ratification.
- Migration of statutes to constitutional protection where appropriate (e.g., ecological ceilings, adversarial-review body, future-generations advocate, audit office, electoral system).
- Sunset on transitional provisions.

### Phase 10: Maturity and Review (Years 12+)

- Periodic constitutional review (TZ-003) cycle.
- Continuous adversarial-review.
- Iteration protocol active for refinements.

## Legal Continuity

The plan respects legal continuity:

- New bodies and rules are established under existing constitutional authority where possible.
- Constitutional amendments follow existing amendment procedures.
- Rights protections cannot be reduced during migration; ratchet only upward.
- Existing institutions are not dissolved; they are reformed or supplemented.

## Institutional Continuity

- Existing civil servants are retained, not purged; offered transition into new roles with support.
- Existing judges' tenure respected; new bodies populated through specified appointment processes.
- Existing political parties remain; party-financing rules apply equally.
- Existing regulators continue under reformed mandates.

## Crisis Robustness During Migration

- All migration steps must hold under crisis (per LAW-E and GOV-D5).
- Migration explicitly designed so that interruption does not produce institutional vacuum: at each phase, the polity has functioning rule of law, rights protection, and elections.
- "Reverse-migration" path defined: if a reform fails, the prior arrangement remains operable with documented transition out.

## Capture Resistance During Migration

Migration is a particularly capture-vulnerable period:

- Reformers can themselves be captured.
- New rules can be designed with hidden loopholes.
- "Necessary urgency" rhetoric can suspend deliberation.

Defenses:

- Decision logs (TAI-O1) operate from Phase 1.
- Adversarial review (GOV-B5) operates from Phase 1.
- Public-deliberation requirement for major reforms.
- External review by independent academic or civil-society bodies.
- Sunset clauses on new instruments to force review.

## Compatibility With Existing Law

Migration accommodates existing legal architecture:

- Federal vs. unitary: subsidiarity rules (TAR-P1) adapt to existing distribution.
- Common-law vs. civil-law: codification practice adapts; precedent or statute as primary depending on tradition.
- Religious-law accommodation: customary-law rules (LAW-S5) within constitutional limits.
- International obligations: existing treaties honored; new treaties pursued aligned with framework values.

## Indicators of Migration Health

- Rights-violation rate declining.
- Judicial independence index rising.
- Press-freedom index rising.
- Trust in institutions rising.
- Corruption-perception declining.
- Public-deliberation activity rising.
- Adversarial-review investigations completed.
- Indicators in TAR-Q on trajectory.

## Failure Modes of Migration

- **Capture of reform process**: reformers captured by concentrated interests; new rules favor incumbents.
- **Speed without legitimacy**: reform passes without sufficient deliberation; coalition collapses.
- **Over-reach**: reformers stretch authority beyond mandate; legitimacy erodes.
- **Adversarial back-lash**: opponents organize before reforms consolidate; reverse adopted reforms.
- **Implementation gap**: laws pass but capacity does not exist to enforce; rules become dead letter (RSK-045).
- **External pressure**: foreign or international actors apply pressure that collapses domestic coalition.

Each failure mode is anticipated and countered:

- Capture: decision logs, adversarial review, conflict-of-interest discipline.
- Speed without legitimacy: phased rollout with deliberation periods.
- Over-reach: judicial review, sunset clauses, public reasoning.
- Back-lash: coalition maintenance, legitimacy investments, capture-resistance audits.
- Implementation gap: capacity building before authority expansion.
- External pressure: alliance work, narrative work, sovereignty rules.

## Mode-Specific Variations

### Mode A (Greenfield)

- Migration plan compressed; constitutional foundation set at founding.
- External-state relationships established at the start.
- Smaller scale permits radical features early but selection bias remains.

### Mode C (Post-Crisis)

- Phase 0 may be skipped or compressed under urgency.
- Truth-and-reconciliation infrastructure established alongside Phase 1.
- International monitoring may be present; framework respects sovereignty but accepts technical assistance.
- Risk of crisis-pressure compromises high; foundational protections must be entrenched early.

## Open Issues

- Sequencing is recommended but not prescriptive; political windows vary.
- Specific costing of each phase depends on starting conditions.
- Coalition dynamics differ by polity; the plan cannot specify them in detail.
- Constitutional-court interpretation of new mechanisms during transition is uncertain; framework relies on courts' good faith and capacity.
- International-law constraints (trade agreements, investment treaties, alliances) may restrict some reforms; renegotiation work required.
