# Adoption Playbook

Artifact ID: ART-025
Iteration: baseline-v0.1
Status: draft
Change type: new
Owner module: all architecture modules
Depends on: ART-024

## Purpose

Provide an operational playbook for community-level and program-level adoption of the framework. Where the roadmap (ART-024) tells what to do in what order at policy scale, the playbook tells how to organize, communicate, and operate week by week and month by month.

The playbook is written for civic actors leading adoption: government reform teams, community founders, civil-society leaders, or transitional administrators. It is descriptive of practice, not legally binding.

## Stakeholder Map

Before any adoption action, identify and engage:

- **Public-sector**: legislatures, executives, judiciaries, civil services, regulators, auditors, ombuds.
- **Civil society**: advocacy organizations, religious bodies, philanthropic foundations, professional bodies, unions, cooperatives, community associations.
- **Business**: dominant firms, SMEs, cooperatives, public enterprises, sectoral associations.
- **Workforce**: organized labor, informal workers, public-service unions.
- **Affected populations**: those harmed by current arrangements, those potentially harmed by reform, vulnerable groups, minorities, indigenous peoples.
- **Adversaries**: those whose interests are damaged by reform; their tactics anticipated.
- **External**: neighbors, allies, multilateral bodies, diaspora.
- **Media**: public-interest, commercial, independent, foreign.

For each, identify:

- Position on each major proposed reform.
- Capacity to support or block.
- Cost of co-optation vs. opposition.
- Channels of communication.

## Communication Strategy

### Principles

- **Lead with values, follow with evidence**: open with what is being protected (dignity, health, freedom, future); then show evidence (ART-004); then describe institutional change.
- **Acknowledge trade-offs honestly**: pretending there are no trade-offs is the fastest way to lose trust.
- **Show the failure mode being avoided**: framing reform as response to specific failure (ART-006) is more durable than utopian framing.
- **Talk about losers and how they will be supported**: just-transition language is operational, not rhetorical.
- **Repeat with patience**: norm shift takes years (B-019); communication is a continuous program.

### Channels

- Public-service broadcasting partnerships.
- Direct member communications through public-channel infrastructure.
- Local community meetings.
- Schools (within EDU-C2 civic-education limits).
- Civil-society partner messaging.
- Online platforms with transparency about reach and engagement.
- Foreign-language and accessible-format coverage.

### Avoid

- One-sided propaganda (counterproductive in pluralistic environment).
- Demonizing opposition (raises temperature, reduces deliberation).
- Hiding tradeoffs (will leak; reduces trust).
- Mandatory mass-media campaigns of single message (raises concerns about state control).

## Coalition Building

### Strategy

- Identify coalition partners who agree on the values even if they disagree on specific mechanisms.
- Build a stable supermajority for foundational changes (rights, observability infrastructure, electoral reform).
- Maintain working majorities for ordinary reforms; sustain across electoral cycles.
- Resist the temptation to push high-controversy reforms in the early window when broader-base support is needed for foundational changes.

### Cross-Cutting Coalitions

Common coalition forms:

- **Anti-corruption coalition**: many interests align against capture and corruption.
- **Care economy coalition**: caregivers, families, eldercare workers, advocates.
- **Ecology + jobs coalition**: just-transition framing.
- **Rights + technology coalition**: pro-privacy, pro-accountability, anti-surveillance.
- **Public health + economy coalition**: health-as-infrastructure framing.
- **Community resilience coalition**: local-cell scale efforts, prepper communities, faith communities, urban-design advocates.

### Adversaries

Anticipate adversarial response:

- **Concentrated wealth** opposes wealth and inheritance taxation, antitrust, land-value tax.
- **Capture-vulnerable industries** oppose regulator independence.
- **Surveillance-state actors** oppose data-rights and observability discipline.
- **Authoritarian foreign actors** may oppose adoption as challenge to their model.
- **Anti-pluralist movements** oppose pluralism protections.
- **Status-quo bureaucracies** resist procedural reform.

Counter-strategies: transparency on opposition financing; legitimacy-building over time; pacing reform to keep coalitions intact; non-escalatory enforcement of new rules.

## Sequencing for Adoption Programs

The roadmap (ART-024) prescribes high-level sequencing. The playbook adds operational sequencing for specific programs.

### Universal Floor + Commons Layer

- Month 0-3: design floor instrument; identify disbursement infrastructure.
- Month 4-12: legislative authorization; pilot in selected regions.
- Year 2: scale-up; commons-layer baseline.
- Year 3-5: full national coverage; calibration to cost of living; review of inflation effects.

### Observability Infrastructure

- Month 0-6: legislative authorization for decision-log requirements; appointment of audit officer for infrastructure.
- Month 6-12: pilot decision-log system in selected agencies; member feedback.
- Year 2: full national decision-log; algorithmic registry.
- Year 3-5: maturity; access-pattern audits; civic engagement programs.

### Adversarial Review Body

- Month 0-6: constitutional or statutory authorization; recruitment of founding members.
- Month 6-18: initial mandate set; first investigations begin.
- Year 2-3: cumulative findings; institutional credibility established.
- Year 4+: standard part of governance landscape.

### Sortition Body

- Month 0-12: legislative authorization; deliberation design; sortition-selection infrastructure.
- Year 1-2: pilot with binding role on selected single issue.
- Year 2-3: regular cycle established.
- Year 5+: durable, with constitutional anchor.

### Ecological Caps

- Month 0-12: science-based baseline; declining-trajectory law.
- Year 2-3: instruments deployed (taxes, auctions, standards).
- Year 5: indicators on trajectory.
- Year 10+: full integration with economic accounts.

### Restorative-Justice Scale-Up

- Month 0-6: pilot in selected jurisdictions for selected offense categories.
- Year 1-3: expansion based on outcomes (CLM-091 design caveat).
- Year 5+: mainstream alternative for eligible cases.

### Land-Value Tax

- Month 0-12: cadastral preparation; valuation methodology; phase-in design.
- Year 2-3: low-rate introduction; assessment cycle.
- Year 5: rate calibration; supporting tax-shift (reducing other taxes proportionally where possible).

## Onboarding Communities (Mode A and Mode B)

### Founding a New Cell (Mode A)

- Charter drafting with future members.
- Resident-selection process: avoid both raw self-selection (which biases the cohort) and forced inclusion (which violates choice).
- Initial governance establishment: provisional council, ombuds, dispute-resolution capacity.
- Universal-services bootstrap: primary care, primary education, basic infrastructure.
- First-year capability and indicator baseline.
- External support agreements: backstop with surrounding jurisdiction; defense and external relations covered by external authority during pilot.

### Reforming an Existing Community (Mode B)

- Local deliberation: explain proposed changes; receive feedback; adapt.
- Voluntary participation in pilot programs: opt-in floor pilot, opt-in capability assessment, opt-in restorative-justice referral.
- Visible early wins: clear infrastructure improvements, service-quality improvements.
- Conflict-resolution capacity built before tensions arise.
- Inter-cell mutual learning: federation of pilot cells share results.

### Integrating New Arrivals (across all modes)

- Reception orientation: rights and obligations; how to access services; how to participate.
- Language and civic-orientation programs.
- Ombuds and conflict-resolution accessibility.
- Cultural-community connection points.
- Anti-discrimination enforcement visible from day one.

## Crisis Management During Adoption

Adoption typically encounters crises (economic shock, electoral cycle, external event). The playbook prescribes:

- **Don't pause foundational protections** (observability, rights protection, adversarial review) under crisis pressure.
- **Do pause non-essential reform pushes** if their failure would discredit foundational protections.
- **Communicate proactively**: silence in crisis is fuel for adversaries.
- **Maintain civil-liberties floor** absolutely.
- **Document everything**: decision logs in crisis are the historical record.
- **Plan post-crisis review**: standing commitment to assess crisis-period decisions afterward.

## Measuring Adoption Progress

- Indicator-panel improvement (TAR-Q).
- Institutional-functioning indicators (e.g., adversarial-review investigations completed; sortition-body output quality; audit-response rate).
- Public-trust trajectory.
- Coalition stability (cross-cycle).
- Capture-resistance audit clean.
- Adversary tactics defeated.
- Communications metrics: comprehension, support, satisfaction.

## Operating Norms for Adoption Teams

- **Decision-log discipline within own team**: lead by example.
- **Conflict-of-interest discipline**: visible compliance with the same anti-capture rules being designed for public institutions.
- **Diversity within team**: avoid the framework being adopted by a narrow group; the team should look like the polity.
- **External feedback loops**: standing advisory panels including critics.
- **Periodic strategic reviews**: every 6 months at adoption phase.
- **Personal care**: adoption work is multi-year and burns out leaders; institutional support for them is required.

## Recovery from Setbacks

Most adoption programs face setbacks. The playbook prescribes:

- **Diagnose the setback**: capture, communication failure, design flaw, external shock.
- **Distinguish between strategic and tactical reversal**: a paused pilot is not a failed program.
- **Maintain coalition continuity**: lose a vote; do not lose the coalition.
- **Update the iteration**: setbacks feed back into the framework's iteration protocol (`canon/iteration-protocol.md`).

## Open Issues

- Adoption is irreducibly political; the playbook cannot replace local political judgment.
- The playbook is biased toward Mode B (reform-by-layers); Mode A (greenfield) and Mode C (post-crisis) deserve dedicated playbooks.
- Stakeholder maps differ by polity; templates here are heuristics, not specifications.
- Cultural variability in deliberation and decision-making norms means playbook tactics need translation in each context.
