# ART-005: Benchmark Catalog

Iteration: `baseline-v0.1`
Status: draft

## Purpose

Catalog historical civilizations, intentional communities, and institutional experiments as reference cases. Each entry identifies what we can learn, what we should not copy, and boundary conditions on the lesson.

## Historical Civilization Benchmarks

### BCM-001: Roman Republic (509-27 BCE)

**Duration**: ~482 years
**Key features**: Mixed constitution (consuls, senate, assemblies), checks and balances, rule of law (Twelve Tables), citizenship expansion.

**What worked**:
- Separation of powers provided resilience for centuries.
- Gradual enfranchisement of allies (after Social War) increased stability.
- Strong civic-religious rituals created shared identity without requiring theological uniformity.

**What failed**:
- Wealth concentration from conquests corrupted electoral politics.
- Professional armies loyal to generals rather than the state enabled coups.
- Elite competition replaced institutional norms as constraints weakened.
- No peaceful constitutional-amendment mechanism led to reform-by-violence.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED] (historical consensus on sequence)
**Sources**: SRC-060, SRC-003
**Boundary conditions**: Pre-industrial agrarian economy; slavery-dependent; no mass communication technology; lessons transfer to institutional design but not anachronistic replication.

**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-ECO, MOD-LAW
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-001, ECO-BCM-001

### BCM-002: Byzantine Empire (330-1453 CE)

**Duration**: ~1,123 years (Eastern Roman phase)
**Key features**: Professional bureaucracy, legal codification (Corpus Juris Civilis), strategic diplomacy, defensive military doctrine, Constantinople's triple-wall defense.

**What worked**:
- Professional, rule-bound bureaucracy with career paths reduced patrimonialism.
- Legal codification under Justinian I provided stable legal framework for centuries.
- Diplomatic corps and intelligence network enabled survival through strategic marriage, tribute, and information advantages rather than pure military force.
- Geographic defense-in-depth and Constantinople's defenses turned geography into strategic asset.

**What failed**:
- Dynastic instability and palace coups recurred throughout.
- Over-reliance on mercenaries created military dependency and fiscal drain.
- Religious schisms and doctrinal disputes became political fractures.
- Fourth Crusade (1204) demonstrated that even the most defensible city falls when internal elite divisions invite external intervention.
- Gradual territorial attrition made the state a city-state before final collapse.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-003, SRC-029
**Boundary conditions**: Theocratic-monarchical; pre-industrial; lessons on bureaucracy, diplomacy, and defensive doctrine transfer; governance model does not.

**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-DEF
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-002, DEF-BCM-001

### BCM-003: Iroquois Confederacy (c. 1142-1779 CE, estimated founding)

**Duration**: ~637 years
**Key features**: Confederated council governance, consensus decision-making, separation of civil and military leadership, clan-based representation, Great Law of Peace (oral constitution).

**What worked**:
- Federal structure with local autonomy and confederal coordination on defense and trade.
- Consensus decision-making with veto rights for each member nation prevented majoritarian tyranny.
- Separation of war chiefs from civil chiefs (peace chiefs) constrained militarization.
- Matrilineal clan system provided stable social organization and leadership selection independent of wealth.
- Oral constitution maintained for centuries through institutionalized recitation.

**What failed**:
- European colonization, disease, and military pressure overwhelmed the confederacy.
- Internal divisions during the American Revolution split the confederacy.
- No scalable model for incorporating large, culturally diverse populations.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED] (ethnohistorical consensus)
**Sources**: SRC-033, SRC-002
**Boundary conditions**: Small-scale (~20,000 at peak); oral-tradition governance; pre-industrial; federal and consensus mechanisms are design-transferable.

**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-CUL
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-003, CUL-BCM-001

### BCM-004: Swiss Confederation (1291-present)

**Duration**: ~732 years (modern federal state since 1848)
**Key features**: Federalism, direct democracy (referendums and initiatives), cantonal autonomy, militia army, armed neutrality, consensus government (Federal Council represents major parties).

**What worked**:
- Extreme subsidiarity: cantons retain all powers not explicitly delegated to federal level.
- Direct democracy provides legitimacy, check on elite capture, and citizen engagement.
- Armed neutrality and universal male conscription (historically) provided effective deterrent without expeditionary military.
- Multi-party executive council with rotating presidency prevents single-person power concentration.
- Multilingual, multi-religious coexistence managed through territorial autonomy rather than assimilation.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Women's suffrage delayed until 1971 (federal level) -- direct democracy can entrench conservative majorities against minority rights.
- Banking secrecy facilitated global tax evasion and money laundering.
- Immigration tensions and 2014 referendum limiting EU migration created treaty conflicts with EU.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-010, SRC-034
**Boundary conditions**: Small wealthy country with defensible geography; unique historical path; direct democracy mechanisms transfer but context matters.

**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-DEF, MOD-CUL
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-004

### BCM-005: Nordic Social Democracies (c. 1930s-present)

**Duration**: ~90+ years (varying by country)
**Key features**: Universal welfare state, high union density, coordinated wage bargaining, high trust, progressive taxation, open economies, strong civil society.

**What worked**:
- Universal programs (not means-tested) built cross-class political coalitions.
- High social trust, low corruption, and strong rule of law co-evolved with welfare state.
- Flexicurity labor markets (especially Denmark) combine employer flexibility with worker security.
- Extensive public investment in education, R&D, and infrastructure.
- High female labor-force participation enabled by public childcare and parental leave.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Integration of immigrant populations remains challenging with labor-market exclusion and social tensions.
- Demographic aging strains pension and healthcare systems.
- Housing affordability crisis in major cities.
- Debate over whether the model is replicable without Nordic cultural homogeneity (though diversity has increased).

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-010, SRC-017, SRC-076
**Boundary conditions**: Small, high-trust, wealthy, relatively homogeneous (historically) societies; welfare-state architecture transfers more readily than cultural preconditions.

**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-ECO, MOD-HMH, MOD-EDU
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-005, ECO-BCM-001

### BCM-006: Singapore (1965-present)

**Duration**: ~60 years
**Key features**: One-party dominant governance, meritocratic bureaucracy, state-capitalist development, public housing (HDB, 80%+ of population), CPF savings system, strict rule of law.

**What worked**:
- Rapid development from third-world to first-world in one generation.
- Public housing created broad-based asset ownership and social stability.
- Technocratic, competence-oriented civil service with competitive pay reduced corruption.
- Ethnic integration quotas in public housing prevented ghettoization.
- Strategic economic planning with continuous adaptation to global markets.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Severe restrictions on press freedom, assembly, and political competition.
- CPF forced savings constrain individual choice and retirement adequacy is debated.
- Migrant-worker exploitation and dependence on low-wage foreign labor.
- Limited civic participation and deliberation outside govt-controlled channels.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-029, SRC-009
**Boundary conditions**: City-state scale; authoritarian-capitalist; lessons on housing policy, bureaucracy, and ethnic-integration mechanisms transfer; governance model's civil-liberties costs are incompatible with VAL-001 and VAL-005.

**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-ECO, MOD-CUL
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-006, ECO-BCM-002

## Intentional Communities and Institutional Experiments

### BCM-007: Mondragon Corporation (1956-present)

**Duration**: ~69 years
**Key features**: Worker-owned cooperative federation, one-worker-one-vote governance, pay ratios historically capped (initially 3:1, later 6-9:1), in-house bank (Caja Laboral), university, and social-security system.

**What worked**:
- Worker ownership aligns incentives, increases productivity, and buffers employment during downturns (members vote for pay cuts rather than layoffs).
- In-house financial institution (now Laboral Kutxa) provided patient capital aligned with cooperative values.
- Survived the 2008 financial crisis and Spanish recession better than conventional firms.
- Currently ~80,000 workers, demonstrating scalability of cooperative model to large scale.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- International subsidiaries are not worker-owned, creating two-tier workforce.
- Pay ratio cap eroded over time as competitive pressure mounted.
- Decision-making can be slower than hierarchical firms.
- Debate over whether success is replicable without Basque cultural solidarity and initial state support.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-002
**Boundary conditions**: Industrial/manufacturing/services cooperatives; lessons on cooperative governance, in-house finance, and employment resilience transfer.

**Relevant to**: MOD-ECO, MOD-GOV
**Module IDs**: ECO-BCM-003

### BCM-008: Israeli Kibbutzim (c. 1910-present, peak ~270 communities)

**Duration**: ~115 years (with major structural changes in 1980s-2000s)
**Key features**: Collective ownership, egalitarian distribution, communal child-rearing (historically), direct-democratic general assembly, rotation of management roles.

**What worked**:
- High levels of social cohesion, trust, and mutual support over decades.
- Produced disproportionate share of Israeli military officers and political leaders, suggesting leadership development.
- Agricultural and later industrial productivity at or above national averages.

**What failed**:
- Economic crisis of the 1980s forced most kibbutzim to privatize partially or fully.
- Communal child-rearing abandoned as parents demanded nuclear-family arrangements.
- Brain drain of younger generations seeking individual economic opportunity.
- Free-rider problems emerged as ideological commitment weakened across generations.
- "Privatized kibbutzim" now operate as hybrid cooperatives, demonstrating limits of pure egalitarianism.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-002
**Boundary conditions**: Small communities (100-1000 people); high-ideology founding generation; lessons on community cohesion and egalitarian design transfers but pure egalitarian model failed under economic and generational pressure.

**Relevant to**: MOD-CUL, MOD-ECO
**Module IDs**: CUL-BCM-002, ECO-BCM-004

### BCM-009: Auroville (1968-present)

**Duration**: ~57 years
**Key features**: Intentional township in Tamil Nadu, India. Non-religious spiritual foundation, collective land ownership, consensus governance, ecological focus, international population (~3,000 residents).

**What worked**:
- Ecological restoration: transformed eroded plateau into forested landscape.
- Diverse international community sustained over decades.
- Innovation in sustainable building, renewable energy, and organic agriculture.
- Community currency and internal economy experiments.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Chronic governance conflicts between residents, the Auroville Foundation (Indian government act), and the Sri Aurobindo Society.
- Land-dispute and encroachment challenges.
- Elite/non-elite divisions between voluntary residents and local Tamil villagers.
- Financial dependence on donations and external funding limits replicability.

**Evidence classification**: [CONTESTED] (documentation quality variable; less systematically studied)
**Sources**: Secondary literature; no primary academic source in register.
**Boundary conditions**: Small-scale; unique legal status under Indian government act; lessons on ecological restoration and intentional-community governance transfer; governance model's unique legal arrangement is non-replicable.

**Relevant to**: MOD-CUL, MOD-ECOINF
**Module IDs**: CUL-BCM-003

---

### BCM-010: Rojava / Democratic Federation of Northern Syria (2012-present)

**Duration**: ~13 years (ongoing, under severe military pressure)
**Key features**: Democratic confederalism, gender-equal co-presidency, commune-level direct democracy, multi-ethnic federation, cooperative economy, women's self-defense units.

**What worked**:
- Maintained governance and military defense through civil war and ISIS conflict.
- Gender parity in all governing bodies is enforced structurally (co-chair system).
- Multi-ethnic coexistence (Kurdish, Arab, Assyrian, Turkmen, Armenian) under shared institutions.
- Rapid deployment of cooperative economy during wartime conditions.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Existence under constant military threat and blockade.
- Economic isolation and dependence on subsistence/humanitarian economy.
- Dependence on US military protection created vulnerability to geopolitical shifts.
- Tensions between democratic confederalist ideology and tribal/patriarchal social structures.
- Displacement of non-Kurdish populations in some areas (contested by observers).

**Evidence classification**: [CONTESTED] (wartime conditions limit data quality; pro/con partisan reporting)
**Sources**: Primary reports; limited academic sourcing in register.
**Boundary conditions**: Wartime revolution; cannot extract peacetime governance lessons without adjustment; gender-parity mechanisms and confederal structure are design-transferable.

**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-CUL, MOD-DEF
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-007

## Political Framework Benchmarks

### BCM-011: Sortition-Based Governance Models

**Historical examples**: Athenian democracy (random selection for Boule, courts), Venetian doge selection (complex lottery), modern citizen assemblies (Ireland constitutional convention, French Climate Convention, British Columbia citizens' assembly).

**Key features**: Random selection from citizenry, deliberation with expert briefing, task-focused (constitutional reform, specific policy area).

**What worked**:
- Irish citizens' assembly (2012-2018) successfully broke political deadlock on same-sex marriage and abortion, leading to constitutional referendums that passed.
- Deliberative polls consistently show opinion shifts toward more informed, nuanced positions.
- Random selection produces more demographically representative bodies than elections.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Not tested as a primary governance mechanism at national scale.
- Risk of capture through framing of questions and selection of expert witnesses.
- Implementation typically requires elected-government sponsorship, creating gatekeeping.
- Unsolved question of accountability: how do citizens recall randomly selected representatives?

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED] for deliberative quality; [THEORETICAL] for primary governance
**Sources**: SRC-090, SRC-095
**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-008

---

### BCM-012: Deliberative Democracy Mechanisms

**Examples**: Participatory budgeting (Porto Alegre, now >3,000 cities), Planning Cells (Germany), Consensus Conferences (Denmark), Deliberative Polling (global).

**What worked**:
- Participatory budgeting in Porto Alegre redirected spending toward poorest districts and reduced clientelism.
- Deliberation reduces polarization when structured with balanced information and facilitated dialogue.
- Citizens can master complex policy information when given adequate time and expert support.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Participatory budgeting in Porto Alegre declined as political leadership changed.
- Scalability: most deliberative mechanisms involve hundreds, not millions.
- "Participation fatigue" when citizens are over-consulted with limited impact.
- Risk of deliberation favoring educated, articulate, and high-status participants unless designed against this.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-095, SRC-090
**Relevant to**: MOD-GOV, MOD-CUL
**Module IDs**: GOV-BCM-009

## Economic Model Benchmarks

### BCM-013: Universal Basic Income (UBI) Experiments

**Examples**: Mincome (Canada, 1970s), GiveDirectly (Kenya, ongoing), Finland (2017-2018), Stockton SEED (2019-2021), multiple US city pilots.

**What worked**:
- Finland: recipients reported improved wellbeing, reduced stress, and slightly higher employment (no employment disincentive).
- Stockton: full-time employment increased 12% among recipients.
- GiveDirectly: cash transfers produce sustained consumption increases, asset accumulation, and psychosocial improvements.
- Hospitalization rates decreased in Mincome experiment (8.5% reduction).

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Finland's narrow design (unemployed only) limits UBI generalization.
- All experiments are time-limited, which shapes behavior differently than permanent policy.
- Macroeconomic funding at national scale remains untested.
- Political coalitions for UBI are fragile (left + libertarian alliance fracturing).

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED] for individual-level effects; [THEORETICAL] for macro-level effects
**Sources**: SRC-099, SRC-054
**Relevant to**: MOD-ECO
**Module IDs**: ECO-BCM-005

---

### BCM-014: Georgist Land Value Taxation

**Historical examples**: Pennsylvania split-rate tax (1913-present, varying), Denmark (1926-present), Estonia, Taiwan, Singapore (land under state ownership with long leases), Hong Kong (pre-1997).

**What worked**:
- Pennsylvania cities using split-rate tax (higher on land, lower on improvements) had measurably more construction and development than comparable cities.
- Singapore's land-lease system captured land-value appreciation for public benefit while enabling efficient land use.
- Denmark's land tax funded local government without distorting development decisions.
- Land value tax is economically efficient (no deadweight loss) -- one of few taxes with near-consensus support from economists across political spectrum.

**What failed (or tensions)**:
- Political difficulty of implementation: landowners are a concentrated, organized interest group.
- Valuation methodology disputes: separating land from improvement value is technically challenging.
- Pennsylvania split-rate tax was partially rolled back after landowner lobbying.
- "Asset-rich, cash-poor" pensioners require deferral mechanisms.

**Evidence classification**: [ESTABLISHED]
**Sources**: SRC-062, SRC-008
**Relevant to**: MOD-ECO, MOD-ECOINF
**Module IDs**: ECO-BCM-006
