# ART-026: Governance Migration Plan

Iteration: `baseline-v0.1`
Status: draft

## Purpose

Specify how to transition from existing governance systems to the framework governance architecture (ART-009). This is the hardest migration challenge because governance is the backbone on which all other modules depend, and governance transition creates the most acute risks of instability, power vacuums, and authoritarian capture.

## Migration Principles

1. **Governance migration must be legitimate**: No coup, no revolution, no imposition. Migration must proceed through democratic processes and constitutional procedures of existing governance systems.
2. **Continuity of essential functions**: Public safety, public health, basic services, defense, and rule of law must continue without interruption during transition.
3. **Incremental, not big-bang**: Transition elements sequentially where possible. Parallel-run old and new systems during transition. Do not shut down old governance before new governance is operational.
4. **Reversible steps**: Each migration step should be reversible if it proves dysfunctional. Avoid irreversible transitions until new systems are validated.
5. **Broad coalition required**: Governance transition cannot succeed with narrow political support. Requires supermajority coalition across political spectrum. Framework must demonstrate value to diverse stakeholders, not just ideological supporters.

## Pre-Migration: Building the Conditions

### Condition 1: Demonstrated Success at Sub-Scale
- Community-cell governance (Tier 1) operational and successful in multiple communities
- Regional federation (Tier 2) operational and successful in pilot network
- Sortition-deliberation mechanisms tested and validated in citizen assemblies at scale
- Cooperative economic governance demonstrated viable at significant scale (Mondragon-scale or larger)
- Observability infrastructure tested and trusted

### Condition 2: Political Consensus
- Broad coalition across political spectrum supports governance reform
- Supermajority public support in opinion research
- Key institutional stakeholders aligned (judiciary, civil service, military, civil society)
- International allies supportive

### Condition 3: Legal and Constitutional Pathway
- Constitutional amendment procedure identified and viable
- Legal framework for new institutions drafted, reviewed, and tested
- Transitional constitutional provisions drafted
- Courts prepared to interpret new constitutional framework

### Condition 4: Institutional Readiness
- Civil service prepared for new governance structures (training, transition planning)
- Sortition infrastructure ready (citizen registry, random-selection system, training programs)
- Electoral system ready for hybrid representation
- Anti-corruption infrastructure (NACA, OIH) ready to activate
- Observability infrastructure ready to deploy

## Migration Phases

### Phase 1: Pre-Transition Reforms (Years 1-5)

Reforms achievable within existing governance frameworks that build toward framework architecture:

1. **Citizen Assemblies**: Establish permanent citizen-assembly mechanism for constitutional and major policy questions. Ireland-style model: random selection, expert briefing, facilitated deliberation, recommendations to legislature/referendum. This builds sortition capacity and public familiarity.

2. **Participatory Budgeting**: Expand from pilot projects to mandatory allocation (10-30% of discretionary budget) at local and regional levels. Builds participatory governance capacity.

3. **Anti-Corruption Reform**: Establish independent anti-corruption body with prosecutorial power and guaranteed budget (NACA-lite within existing constitutional framework). Builds anti-capture infrastructure.

4. **Transparency and Open Data**: Comprehensive government transparency. All budgets, procurement, decisions, and performance data publicly accessible. Builds observability culture.

5. **Electoral Reform**: If not already: proportional representation, public campaign financing, independent electoral commission. Builds toward hybrid-representation model.

6. **Judicial Independence Strengthening**: Non-renewable long terms for high-court judges. Independent nominating commissions. Indexed judicial budgets.

7. **Local Governance Empowerment**: Devolution of powers to local level. Subsidiarity principle in law. Builds toward tiered governance.

**Decision**: DEC-MIG-001 (pre-transition sequencing). Rationale: These reforms are achievable within existing constitutional frameworks (many with existing precedents). They build institutional capacity, public familiarity, and evidence base for more fundamental transition. They are individually valuable even if full transition never occurs.

---

### Phase 2: Constitutional Transition (Years 5-10)

Fundamental governance redesign through constitutional process:

1. **Constitutional Convention**: Convention with mixed representation (elected + sortition + expert, per target model). Mandate: draft new constitutional framework based on ART-008 and ART-009. Process: 1-2 years of deliberation, expert testimony, public consultation, adversarial review.

2. **Public Deliberation**: Draft constitution subjected to extensive public deliberation. Citizen assemblies in every region. Public comment period. Deliberative polling. Media coverage and public education.

3. **Ratification**: National referendum. Threshold: 60%+ approval AND majority in majority of regions (federal protection). If failed, return to Phase 1 reforms.

4. **Transitional Provisions**:
   - Existing institutions continue until new institutions operational
   - Phased transfer of powers by institution
   - Constitutional Court transition: staggered terms so continuity maintained
   - Existing laws remain in force until amended or repealed under new constitution
   - Rights protections in existing constitution continue (no gap)
   - International treaty obligations continue

5. **First Elections Under New System**:
   - Mixed-representation National Assembly (electoral + sortition + expert)
   - Regional Assemblies
   - Community Assemblies activated

6. **New Institutions Activation** (in order):
   a. Constitutional Court (first, to provide constitutional review)
   b. National Audit and Anti-Corruption Authority (NACA)
   c. Observatory of Institutional Health (OIH)
   d. National Assembly and Executive
   e. Regional Assemblies (concurrent)
   f. Community Assemblies (concurrent)
   g. Constitutional Review Commission (adversarial review body)

---

### Phase 3: Deep Institutionalization (Years 10-20)

Full implementation and maturation:

1. **Judicial System Transition**: New judicial-appointment procedures. Community courts activated. Specialized tribunals established (environmental, technology, etc.).

2. **Civil Service Transition**: Competence-gating for technical roles. Performance-audit systems. Rotation and term-limit norms established.

3. **Observability Full Deployment**: All institutional processes observable. Real-time dashboards public. Privacy safeguards tested and trusted.

4. **Amendment Process Activation**: First constitutional amendments processed through new amendment procedure (VAL-018). Test of self-correction mechanism.

5. **Defense Governance**: Civilian control institutionalized under new framework. Parliamentary oversight committees functional. Military oath to constitution operationalized.

6. **International Recognition**: New governance architecture recognized internationally. Treaty continuity confirmed. Diplomatic relations maintained.

## Migration Risk Assessment

| Risk | Severity | Probability | Mitigation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Transition creates power vacuum | 9 | Medium | Phased transition; existing institutions continue until new ones operational |
| Constitutional convention captured by special interests | 8 | Medium | Mixed representation (sortition + electoral + expert); transparent process; public deliberation |
| Public rejection of new constitution | 7 | Medium | High threshold for ratification (60%+) builds legitimacy but creates rejection risk; Phase 1 reforms continue if rejected |
| Civil-service resistance | 6 | Medium | Inclusion in design process; job security during transition; competence-gating as opportunity, not threat |
| Military resistance | 9 | Low-Medium | Gradual transition; military consulted in defense-governance design; constitutional oath; alliance integration as external stabilizer |
| Economic disruption during constitutional uncertainty | 7 | Medium | Clear timeline; investment protections; central-bank independence maintained |
| International pressure or intervention | 8 | Low | Alliance relationships maintained; international legal continuity; diplomatic engagement throughout |
| Elite sabotage (those who lose power under new system) | 7 | Medium-High | Transition compensation (pensions, honors, roles) for displaced elites; broad coalition reduces elite blocking power |
| Regional secession attempt | 8 | Medium | Federal structure grants autonomy; constitutional secession procedure with high threshold; economic integration increases secession cost |

## Migration Governance

### Transition Authority
A temporary Transition Governance Commission oversees the migration:
- **Composition**: Mixed (elected, sortition, expert, judicial, civil-society)
- **Powers**: Coordination and oversight of transition, not governance (existing governance continues)
- **Duration**: 5 years from constitutional ratification, with possible 2-year extension
- **Sunset**: Automatically dissolves after transition complete
- **Accountability**: Reports to Constitutional Convention during drafting, then to National Assembly

### Transition Monitoring
- Observatory of Institutional Health provisionally activated to monitor migration
- Public dashboard of transition progress
- Civil-society monitoring and reporting
- International observation (similar to election observation)

## Non-Democratic Context Migration

In non-democratic contexts, governance migration faces fundamentally different challenges:

1. **Framework may be aspirational, not immediately implementable**: Democratic governance transition in authoritarian contexts requires regime change, which is beyond this framework's scope.

2. **Sub-governance adoption**: Even in non-democratic contexts, elements of the framework can be adopted at sub-governance levels: cooperatives, community land trusts, mutual-aid networks, deliberative practices within permitted civil-society space.

3. **Long-term horizon**: Governance migration in non-democratic contexts is generational, not policy-cycle.

4. **Caution**: The framework must never be used to justify foreign intervention or regime-change operations. Adoption must be endogenous.
