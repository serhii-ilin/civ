# ART-025: Adoption Playbook

Iteration: `baseline-v0.1`
Status: draft

## Purpose

A practical guide for communities, organizations, and eventually governments seeking to adopt elements of the civilization framework. This is the "how to start" document, complementing the "what and when" of the implementation roadmap (ART-024).

## Playbook Principles

1. **Start small, learn fast**: Begin with what you can implement now. Build evidence. Expand from demonstrated success.
2. **Voluntary, never coercive**: Adoption must be through consent, demonstration, and attraction -- never mandate or force.
3. **Adapt to context**: The framework is a template, not a blueprint. Communities adapt it to local culture, ecology, economy, and legal context.
4. **Open-source the learning**: Share what works and what fails. The framework improves through distributed learning.
5. **Build coalitions**: Framework adoption is social and political, not just technical. Build relationships across diverse stakeholders.

## Starting Points

### For Individuals

**What one person can do now**:
1. **Learn**: Study the framework. Understand the values, evidence, and architecture.
2. **Connect**: Find others interested in alternative social/economic/governance models.
3. **Practice locally**: Join or start cooperative enterprises. Participate in community governance. Practice deliberative democracy skills. Support local food systems.
4. **Advocate**: Make the case for framework elements (land-value tax, universal services, cooperative economy, sortition governance) in existing political processes.
5. **Prepare**: Develop skills useful in framework communities (facilitation, mediation, cooperative management, regenerative agriculture, community health).

### For Existing Communities

**What neighborhoods, towns, or intentional communities can adopt now**:

1. **Community Assembly**: Start regular community meetings with facilitated deliberation. Not (yet) formal governance -- building the practice.
2. **Mutual Aid Network**: Organize neighborhood pods for daily support (childcare sharing, elder checks, emergency help, skill sharing).
3. **Community Garden / Food Cooperative**: Start local food production and distribution.
4. **Tool Library / Repair Cafe**: Build sharing economy and circular-economy practices.
5. **Community Mediation**: Train community mediators. Offer mediation for neighborhood disputes.
6. **Community Land Trust**: If acquiring land, use CLT model for permanent affordability.
7. **Observability Dashboard**: Track community wellbeing metrics. Make them visible. Start simple.
8. **Cooperative Enterprise**: Convert existing business to worker cooperative or start new cooperative.

### For Organizations

**What cooperatives, nonprofits, and businesses can adopt**:

1. **Worker ownership**: Convert to worker cooperative or multi-stakeholder cooperative governance.
2. **Pay-ratio limits**: Cap executive-to-worker pay ratio at 20:1 or lower.
3. **Ecological accounting**: Measure and reduce ecological footprint. Implement circular-economy practices.
4. **Democratic workplace**: Implement participatory decision-making. Elect leadership. Rotate management roles.
5. **Community benefit**: Legally commit to community benefit (B Corp certification, community interest company).
6. **Transparency**: Open-book management. Public reporting on social and ecological impact.

## Community Cell Startup Playbook

### Pre-Launch Phase (6-12 months)

1. **Form Core Group** (8-15 committed founders)
   - Shared commitment to framework values
   - Diverse skills: governance, facilitation, finance, agriculture, construction, health, education, mediation, technology
   - Clear decision-making process for founding phase
   - Agreement on founding principles and exit procedures

2. **Legal and Financial Foundation**
   - Establish legal entity (cooperative, community interest company, trust)
   - Secure land (purchase with CLT structure or long-term lease)
   - Raise seed capital (member contributions, community shares, ethical investment, philanthropy)
   - Develop financial model: 5-year projected income (member contributions, enterprise revenue, grants) and expenses

3. **Site Selection and Design**
   - Criteria: ecological capacity, legal permissibility, proximity to services, community interest
   - Design: per ART-015 spatial-design principles (walkable, mixed-use, green space, community commons)
   - Infrastructure planning: energy (renewable microgrid), water, food, housing, communications

4. **Community Building**
   - Recruit members (transparent about commitment, expectations, and exit rights)
   - Pre-move-in community-building activities (workshops, shared meals, collaborative planning)
   - Develop community agreements: governance, conflict resolution, resource sharing, privacy, observability
   - Train facilitators, mediators, and Community Health Coordinator

### Launch Phase (Year 1)

1. **Governance Activation**
   - First Community Assembly within 30 days of reaching minimum population
   - Elect/sortition first Community Council
   - Establish community budget and participatory budgeting process
   - Activate community observability dashboard

2. **Economic Foundation**
   - Launch cooperative enterprises (at least 2-3 to provide employment diversity)
   - Establish community currency or local exchange system (optional)
   - Connect to regional cooperative federation
   - Food system: community gardens, orchards, food cooperative

3. **Infrastructure**
   - Housing: complete first residences
   - Energy: microgrid operational
   - Water: secure supply and treatment
   - Communications: community network
   - Common spaces: community hall, kitchen, workshop, gathering spaces

4. **Care Infrastructure**
   - Community Health Coordinator active
   - Community Mental Health Facilitator active
   - Mutual-aid network operational
   - Childcare cooperative launched
   - Elder support activated

5. **Education**
   - If families with children: community school or homeschool cooperative
   - Adult education: skill-sharing workshops, civic education, deliberation training

### Growth and Maturation (Year 2-5)

1. **Governance Maturation**
   - Refine assembly and council processes based on experience
   - Develop inter-community relationships (sister communities, regional coordination)
   - First adversarial review (community self-assessment)

2. **Economic Maturation**
   - Enterprises reach sustainable profitability
   - Community approaching financial self-sufficiency
   - Member livelihoods stabilized

3. **Cultural Development**
   - Community rituals and celebrations established
   - Arts and cultural life flourishing
   - Intergenerational connections strong

4. **Evidence Generation**
   - Document what works and what doesn't
   - Measure against MVP success criteria
   - Publish findings openly
   - Contribute to framework iteration

## Adoption by Sector

### Health Sector
- **Now**: Integrate physical and mental health. Adopt trauma-informed practices. Build community health worker networks.
- **Transition**: Move toward universal single-payer. Establish independent public-health agency.

### Education Sector
- **Now**: Reduce standardized testing. Increase project-based and civic education. Train teachers in trauma-informed practice.
- **Transition**: Move toward portfolio assessment. Integrate SEL. Establish lifelong learning entitlement.

### Governance Sector
- **Now**: Implement participatory budgeting. Experiment with citizen assemblies on specific issues. Adopt transparency and open-data practices.
- **Transition**: Move toward mixed-representation model. Establish independent anti-corruption body. Implement constitutional self-correction.

### Economy Sector
- **Now**: Support cooperative conversions. Implement local procurement preferences. Start community land trusts.
- **Transition**: Move toward land-value taxation. Implement universal basic services. Adopt beyond-GDP metrics.

## Coalition Building

Framework adoption requires broad coalitions. Potential coalition partners:

- **Cooperative movement**: Worker cooperatives, consumer cooperatives, credit unions, agricultural cooperatives, housing cooperatives
- **Environmental movement**: Climate action, biodiversity, circular economy, regenerative agriculture
- **Social-justice movement**: Economic inequality, racial justice, gender equity, disability rights, care-economy advocates
- **Democracy-reform movement**: Campaign-finance reform, ranked-choice voting, citizen assemblies, deliberative democracy
- **Community-development movement**: Community land trusts, participatory budgeting, community wealth building
- **Health-reform movement**: Universal healthcare, mental-health parity, social determinants of health
- **Education-reform movement**: Whole-child education, democratic schools, lifelong learning
- **Technology-ethics movement**: Data rights, AI governance, platform accountability, digital privacy
- **Peace and disarmament movement**: Nuclear disarmament, arms control, peaceful conflict resolution
- **Spiritual and religious communities**: Those aligned with framework values of dignity, care, stewardship, and community

These groups do not currently see themselves as part of a unified civilizational project. The framework's role is to show how their individual goals are interconnected parts of a coherent architecture, and how working together achieves what none can achieve alone.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Perfectionism**: Waiting until everything is figured out before starting. Start with what's possible now. Learn through doing.
2. **Founder syndrome**: Founding members monopolize leadership and resist rotation. Sortition and term limits from day one.
3. **Ideological purity**: Expelling those who disagree on non-core issues. The framework values pluralism (VAL-010). Thin consensus, thick diversity.
4. **Financial naivety**: Assuming good intentions replace sound economics. Cooperative enterprises must be competitive. Commons-layer services must be funded.
5. **Scale mismatch**: Trying to implement national-level architecture at community scale, or assuming community-scale success automatically scales.
6. **Ignoring legal context**: Operating outside law creates vulnerability. Build legal structures, seek legal recognition, advocate for legal reform.
7. **Burnout**: Founding requires intense commitment. Design for sustainability from day one. Care for caregivers. Rotation of demanding roles.
8. **Insularity**: Becoming closed community that loses connection to wider society. Maintain external engagement. Accept new members. Share learning.
