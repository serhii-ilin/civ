# ART-024: Implementation Roadmap

Iteration: `baseline-v0.1`
Status: draft

## Purpose

Define the path from architectural specification to operational society. This roadmap is necessarily speculative -- implementing a new civilization framework has no direct precedent. The roadmap prioritizes safety, reversibility, evidence-building, and consensus-building over speed.

## Minimum Viable Pilot (MVP)

### Design
A limited-scale pilot implementing core mechanisms in a voluntary community context. Not a "new country" but a bounded test environment.

**Scope**:
- **Population**: 500-2,000 voluntary participants
- **Geography**: Single site or networked community cells
- **Duration**: 5-year minimum pilot with renewable commitment
- **Modules tested**: Governance (Tier 1 community cell), economy (commons layer + cooperative enterprises), community/culture, ecological infrastructure, health (community health network), education (primary phase)

**Not tested at pilot scale**: National governance, defense, monetary policy, international relations, large-scale economic dynamics, mass migration, simulation-stress scenarios requiring scale.

**Legal framework**:
- Operates within existing national legal system
- Supplementary governance through voluntary association and contractual agreements
- Community land trust or long-term lease for land
- Cooperative legal structures for economic enterprises
- Mutual-aid agreements for commons-layer services

### MVP Success Criteria
1. Community Assembly functions (participation >60%, satisfaction >70%)
2. Cooperative enterprises viable (productivity within 10% of conventional comparators)
3. Commons-layer services delivered effectively (quality metrics at or above national benchmarks)
4. Conflict resolution at community level (<10% escalation to formal legal system)
5. Wellbeing metrics at or above regional baseline
6. No exit barriers (people can leave freely; exit rate not indicating dissatisfaction)
7. Ecological footprint below regional per-capita average
8. Financial sustainability (not dependent on external donations beyond startup)

## Adoption Phases

### Phase 1: Foundation Building (Year 1-5)

**Objective**: Establish MVP, build evidence base, develop legal and institutional templates, grow community of practice.

**Activities**:
- Establish pilot community (one or more cells)
- Develop cooperative enterprise startup support infrastructure
- Create Community Land Trust legal templates
- Train community mediators, facilitators, and health coordinators
- Develop and test sortition and deliberative democracy protocols
- Establish community observability infrastructure
- Document and publish learning
- Build network of interested communities and researchers
- Develop legal-strategy playbook for communities operating within existing jurisdictions

**Governance dependencies**: Minimal. Operates within existing legal frameworks. Primary need: local-government permission and cooperation.

**Economic dependencies**: Seed funding for land acquisition, infrastructure, and startup costs. Philanthropic or impact-investment capital. Self-sufficiency target within 5 years.

**Risk**: Pilot may demonstrate that elements work in small, self-selected, high-commitment community but not at scale. Mitigation: explicitly test for self-selection effects; run parallel studies with less-selected populations.

---

### Phase 2: Network Building (Year 5-10)

**Objective**: Scale from single pilot to network of communities. Test inter-community coordination and regional governance (Tier 2).

**Activities**:
- Establish 10-20 community cells networked into pilot regional federation
- Implement regional governance (Regional Assembly, Executive Council, Court)
- Develop inter-community resource-sharing and mutual-aid protocols
- Test regional economic coordination (cooperative federation, regional procurement)
- Establish regional health and education systems
- Develop community-cell replication methodology (not just growth, but new-cell creation)
- Implement regional observability infrastructure
- Publish comprehensive evidence base from 5+ years of pilot data
- Begin advocacy for legal frameworks that support cooperative and community governance

**Governance dependencies**: Regional-coordination legal structures. May require special legal designations or agreements with regional/provincial government.

**Economic dependencies**: Expanded funding. Cooperative banking infrastructure. Regional economic viability must approach self-sufficiency.

**Risk**: Scaling may surface conflicts absent at single-community scale (resource competition, cultural divergence between communities). Mitigation: regional governance with strong subsidiarity and explicit dispute-resolution mechanisms.

---

### Phase 3: Systemic Adoption (Year 10-20)

**Objective**: Transition from demonstration projects to systematic adoption at regional or national scale. This is the inflection point where voluntary communities become societal architecture.

**Activities**:
- Constitutional convention if pursuing national-level adoption
- Legislative and regulatory reform to enable framework institutions (land-value taxation, cooperative legal structures, universal services, sortition governance)
- Phased transition of existing institutions (welfare state -> commons layer; corporate economy -> cooperative ecosystem)
- National observability infrastructure
- Defense and external-relations architecture activated if sovereign
- Monetary and fiscal policy transition
- Full simulation validation informing transition decisions

**Governance dependencies**: Major legal and constitutional change. Requires democratic mandate (referendum, constitutional convention, legislative supermajority). Cannot be imposed.

**Economic dependencies**: Full economic system transition. Tax reform. Monetary policy if sovereign. Transition costs significant. Requires broad political and economic coalition.

**Risk**: Transition disruption -- existing systems break down before new systems are functional. Mitigation: phased, parallel-run transition; maintain legacy systems during new-system implementation; comprehensive stress testing; explicit rollback mechanisms.

---

### Phase 4: Maturation (Year 20+)

**Objective**: Framework becomes the operational architecture of society. Continuous improvement through self-correction mechanisms (VAL-018).

**Activities**:
- Full deployment of all modules (governance, economy, law, health, education, community, ecology, technology, defense)
- Constitutional amendment procedures active
- Permanent adversarial review functioning
- Generational transmission of civic values through education and culture
- International alliances and external relations established
- Simulation used for ongoing policy testing and risk assessment
- Ecosystem of communities with diverse implementations of shared framework

**Governance dependencies**: Stable constitutional order. Functioning amendment and self-correction mechanisms.

**Economic dependencies**: Mature commons-layer + cooperative + entrepreneurial economy. Established beyond-GDP metrics and ecological accounting.

**Risk**: Generational value drift (RSK-007), institutional entropy, and emergent dysfunctions not anticipated in original design. Mitigation: self-correction mechanisms (VAL-018), ongoing simulation, adversarial review.

## Community Onboarding

### From Early Adopter to Mainstream

1. **Innovators (0-2%)**: Ideologically committed founders. Pilot community members.
2. **Early Adopters (2-15%)**: Attracted by demonstrated success. Form second-wave communities.
3. **Early Majority (15-50%)**: Join when framework is proven and mainstream-accessible. Requires reduced commitment barriers (can participate without being a founder). This is the scaling inflection point.
4. **Late Majority (50-85%)**: Join when framework becomes default/normative.
5. **Laggards (85-100%)**: May never voluntarily join. Framework must accommodate those who prefer existing systems (VAL-010 pluralism). Coercion prohibited.

### Onboarding Support
- Community-cell startup toolkit: legal templates, governance training, infrastructure planning, financial models
- Migration support: housing assistance, employment transition, community integration
- Digital platform: connect aspiring community members, match skills to community needs, share best practices
- Continuous learning: community of practice, annual gathering, research dissemination

## Exit and Rollback Mechanisms

### Individual Exit
- Any person may leave any community at any time
- Fair buyout of community-shared assets
- Relocation support
- Exit right is fundamental (VAL-001, VAL-010). No community may trap members.

### Community Dissolution
- Communities may dissolve by supermajority vote (>60%)
- Asset distribution: community land trust assets remain in trust for successor community; cooperative assets distributed to members per cooperative bylaws
- Member relocation support

### Framework Rollback (Phase 3+)
- Constitutional amendment procedure can modify or replace framework institutions
- Supermajority requirement (65%+) in both deliberative assembly and citizen referendum
- Phased rollback, not abrupt -- maintain institutional continuity during transition
- If framework fails (systemic collapse, democratic rejection), return to prior constitutional order or democratically chosen alternative

## Critical Unknowns

The implementation roadmap is the most speculative artifact in the framework. Key unknowns:

1. **Political feasibility**: Can democratic processes produce the constitutional and legal changes required? Historical precedent for fundamental governance redesign through democratic means is limited.

2. **Transition economics**: How much does it cost to transition from current economic system to framework economy? Over what period? Who bears the cost?

3. **Voluntary vs. mandated adoption**: Can a framework achieve sufficient scale through voluntary adoption? If not, democratic mandate is required -- but can democracies mandate fundamental social change?

4. **Opposition**: Who opposes this framework and with what resources? Democratic change requires political coalitions. What coalition supports this?

5. **Unintended consequences**: What problems emerge at scale that are invisible at pilot scale and in simulation?

These unknowns are not failures of the architecture but honest acknowledgments of the gap between design and reality. The roadmap itself calls for phased, evidence-building, reversible implementation precisely because these unknowns exist.
