# ART-008: Value Constitution

Iteration: `baseline-v0.1`
Status: draft

## Preamble

This Value Constitution defines the foundational principles that constrain and guide all institutional design in the civilization framework. It is derived from `canon/values.md` but is expanded here to include formal definitions, quantifiable indicators where possible, explicit tradeoff recognition, and conflict-resolution procedures.

Values are ordered by scope, not priority. When values conflict, the Conflict Resolution Rules (Section 3) apply. No value is absolute; all are constrained by all others.

---

## Section 1: Core Values (Formal Definitions)

### VAL-001: Human Dignity and Rights

**Formal definition**: Every person possesses inherent worth that cannot be reduced to economic output, social utility, or group membership. This worth entails negative rights (freedom from arbitrary coercion, torture, slavery, surveillance, censorship, and extrajudicial punishment) and positive rights (access to legal representation, due process, freedom of conscience, freedom of association, freedom of movement, and protection of bodily autonomy).

**Key indicators**:
- Civil-liberties index (e.g., V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index)
- Due-process access rate (% of people who can access legal representation within X days)
- Rights-violation rate per 100,000 (torture, arbitrary detention, forced disappearance)
- Minority-rights protection score
- Bodily-autonomy violation rate

**Non-derogable elements**: Freedom from torture, extrajudicial killing, slavery, and forced disappearance. All other rights may be subject to proportionate limitation during declared emergencies with mandatory review.

**Evidence**: CLM-001 (inclusive institutions), CLM-029 (independent courts). Sources: SRC-001, SRC-005, SRC-045.

---

### VAL-002: Health by Design

**Formal definition**: Physical, mental, relational, and societal health are not downstream outcomes of prosperity but structural design requirements. Every institutional, economic, ecological, and social design decision must consider its health impact. Health is defined broadly: absence of preventable disease, mental wellbeing, relational health, community health, and environmental health.

**Key indicators**:
- Life expectancy at birth
- Healthy life years (HALE)
- Preventable disease burden (DALYs per 100,000 from preventable causes)
- Mental-health prevalence (depression, anxiety, substance-use disorder rates)
- Loneliness index (% population reporting frequent loneliness)
- Childhood ACE prevalence rate

**Design constraint**: Policy decisions with significant health impact require health-impact assessment equivalent to current environmental-impact requirements in leading jurisdictions.

**Evidence**: CLM-009 (ACEs), CLM-006 (scarcity/cognition), CLM-022 (universal healthcare), CLM-041 (housing/health). Sources: SRC-019, SRC-012, SRC-013, SRC-018.

---

### VAL-003: Ecological Boundaries

**Formal definition**: All economic, industrial, agricultural, and social activity must operate within scientifically established planetary and local ecological limits. These boundaries are not aspirational targets but hard constraints, with automatic triggers when approached.

**Key indicators**:
- Material footprint per capita (tons/year)
- Carbon budget (cumulative CO2-eq emissions vs. 1.5C/2C budgets)
- Biodiversity impact (Mean Species Abundance, extinction rate relative to background)
- Water stress (% of renewable freshwater withdrawn)
- Land-use intensity (% of land converted from natural state)
- Nitrogen/phosphorus boundary transgression status

**Planetary boundaries**: Adopt the Rockström et al. (2009) / Steffen et al. (2015) framework. Currently 6 of 9 boundaries are transgressed globally; the framework must operate within these constraints regardless of global non-compliance.

**Evidence**: CLM-005 (planetary boundaries). Sources: SRC-020, SRC-021, SRC-022, SRC-057.

---

### VAL-004: Resilience as Architecture

**Formal definition**: The society must be designed to survive, adapt to, and recover from ecological, economic, military, technological, institutional, and cultural shocks. Resilience is not an emergency-preparedness add-on but a structural property of every system: redundant, modular, adaptable, and capable of graceful degradation rather than catastrophic failure.

**Key indicators**:
- Recovery time from shock events (time to return to 90% of pre-shock function)
- Reserve capacity (food, energy, water, medical supplies measured in months of autarky)
- System redundancy (n+1 for critical infrastructure; n+2 for existentially critical)
- Supply-chain diversity (Herfindahl index of import concentration)
- Institutional continuity (whether governance functions survive crisis)
- Adaptability index (rate of institutional, economic, and technological adaptation)

**Design constraint**: Every critical system must have a manual/degraded-mode fallback independent of its digital/automated primary mode.

**Evidence**: CLM-003 (complexity collapse debated), CLM-030 (community resilience), CLM-035 (multi-factor collapse). Sources: SRC-003, SRC-027, SRC-002.

### VAL-005: Legitimate and Consent-Based Governance

**Formal definition**: Governance authority derives from and depends upon the ongoing consent of the governed. Legitimacy requires that governance be (a) competent, delivering public goods effectively; (b) fair, treating citizens equally and predictably; (c) contestable, allowing peaceful challenge and removal of office-holders; (d) participatory, providing genuine avenues for citizen influence; and (e) transparent, with public decisions and their rationales open to scrutiny.

**Key indicators**:
- Trust in institutions (% population expressing confidence in government, judiciary, police)
- Electoral participation (% voting-age population voting in most recent elections)
- Non-electoral participation (% engaging in citizen assembly, participatory budgeting, etc.)
- Appeal and review success rates (% of challenged decisions upheld on merits)
- Perceived procedural fairness (survey-based)
- Peaceful power-transfer track record (years since last non-peaceful transfer)

**Evidence**: CLM-011 (deliberative democracy), CLM-025 (peaceful transfer), CLM-042 (hybrid systems). Sources: SRC-090, SRC-010, SRC-029.

### VAL-006: Capture-Resistant Governance

**Formal definition**: Governance must be structurally resistant to capture by wealthy individuals, corporations, interest groups, foreign powers, criminal organizations, religious institutions, or internal bureaucratic cliques. Capture resistance is a design property, not a policy preference -- it must operate even when power-holders themselves wish to be captured.

**Key indicators**:
- Power-concentration metrics (wealth share of top 0.1%, 1%, 10% at each governance tier)
- Conflict-of-interest violations (per year, per institution)
- Lobbying-transparency score (all meetings with public officials logged and published)
- Regulatory-capture assessment (independent review of regulatory outcomes vs. public interest)
- Revolving-door compliance (% of officials respecting post-employment restrictions)
- Enforcement independence (prosecutorial and audit-body autonomy score)

**Structural anti-capture mechanisms required**:
- Public campaign finance (no private donations to electoral campaigns)
- Independent anti-corruption body with prosecutorial powers
- Real-time public asset declarations for all office-holders
- Competitive procurement with mandatory open bidding
- Post-employment cooling-off periods (minimum 2 years for senior officials)

**Evidence**: CLM-014 (regulatory capture), CLM-043 (structural anti-corruption). Sources: SRC-011, SRC-034.

---

### VAL-007: Competence with Accountability

**Formal definition**: Every role with public authority requires verified competence, ethical fitness, and transparent accountability. Authority without verified capability causes harm; capability without accountability enables abuse. Selection, review, and removal mechanisms must be transparent, fair, and resistant to both credential cartels and populist anti-expertise movements.

**Key indicators**:
- Qualification-verification rate (roles where competence is assessed by independent body)
- Performance-audit coverage (% of authority-bearing roles audited annually)
- Removal/recall rate where justified (neither too low nor weaponized)
- Error-correction time (mean time between detection of material error and institutional correction)
- Merit-vs-patronage ratio in public-sector appointments

**Design constraint**: "Competence-gated" means access to technical authority roles requires demonstrated capability through transparent, appealable assessment. It must not become a credential cartel or class filter.

**Evidence**: CLM-049 (stable systems share institutional features including competence), CLM-040 (elite overrepresentation). Sources: SRC-029, SRC-010.

### VAL-008: Justice and Rule of Law

**Formal definition**: Law must be public, prospective, consistent, rights-protecting, proportionate, and accessible to all persons under jurisdiction. No one -- citizen, official, or institution -- is above the law. Justice must be timely, fair, and equally accessible regardless of wealth, status, identity, or connections.

**Key indicators**:
- Legal-access rate (% who can obtain legal representation when needed)
- Case-processing time (median time from filing to resolution by case type)
- Wrongful-conviction rate (per 100,000 convicted)
- Excessive-force complaints per 1,000 police-public interactions
- Restorative-justice resolution rate (% of eligible cases resolved restoratively)
- Recidivism rate post-resolution (by justice pathway)

**Evidence**: CLM-029 (independent courts reduce corruption and protect rights). Sources: SRC-001, SRC-010, SRC-032.

### VAL-009: Science-Guided Policy

**Formal definition**: Empirical questions in public policy should be answered by the best available evidence, transparently communicated with quantified uncertainty. Value-laden tradeoffs remain democratically and constitutionally governed. Science advises; it does not command. But when science clearly indicates danger, precautionary action is warranted.

**Key indicators**:
- Evidence-grading compliance (% of major policy decisions with documented evidence basis)
- Replication status of key policy-relevant findings
- Policy-review cadence (% of evidence-based policies reviewed within N years)
- Uncertainty disclosure in public communications (% of government statements on empirical matters that quantify uncertainty)

**Design constraint**: Scientific advisory bodies must be independent of political control (similar to central-bank independence model), publicly report their advice, and be subject to adversarial scientific review.

**Evidence**: CLM-017 (automation debated), CLM-005 (planetary boundaries), CLM-026 (pluralism theoretical). Sources: SRC-020, SRC-022, SRC-075.

### VAL-010: Pluralism and Freedom of Conscience

**Formal definition**: The society must protect religious, spiritual, philosophical, cultural, linguistic, and lifestyle diversity. No belief system, cultural practice, or identity group may claim institutional supremacy. Pluralism includes the right to hold beliefs, practice them (within bounds of other values), change them, and raise children in them -- and also the right to be free from them.

**Key indicators**:
- Viewpoint-diversity score (measured through content analysis, survey)
- Discrimination-rate by group (perceived and documented)
- Exit rights (proportion of people able to leave belief communities without penalty)
- Cultural-participation diversity (Herfindahl index of cultural-production participation)
- Minority-language vitality index

**Evidence**: CLM-026 (pluralistic societies more peaceful and innovative -- theoretical). Sources: SRC-010, SRC-031, SRC-089.

---

### VAL-011: Education as Foundation

**Formal definition**: Education is a lifelong right and the foundation of individual flourishing and civic competence. It develops cognitive resilience, emotional intelligence, practical capability, scientific literacy, civic knowledge, and the capacity for self-directed learning. Education must not reproduce class hierarchies through credential sorting or debt.

**Key indicators**:
- Literacy and numeracy rates (by age cohort, not just overall)
- Critical-thinking assessment scores (standardized, externally validated)
- Civic-knowledge scores (understanding of governance, rights, responsibilities)
- Lifelong-learning participation (% adults engaged in structured learning annually)
- Educational-mobility index (correlation between parent and child educational attainment)
- Reskilling-access rate (% of displaced workers accessing effective retraining)

**Evidence**: CLM-021 (credentialism/class reproduction), CLM-008 (SDT -- autonomy, competence, relatedness). Sources: SRC-052, SRC-016.

---

### VAL-012: Mental Health as Civic Infrastructure

**Formal definition**: Psychological wellbeing is not solely an individual healthcare responsibility but a matter of public infrastructure and institutional design. The built environment, economic security, social connection, institutional fairness, and cultural norms all affect population mental health. Treating mental illness downstream while ignoring upstream structural determinants is both ineffective and unjust.

**Key indicators**:
- Mental-healthcare access (% with unmet mental-health needs)
- Suicide rate (per 100,000)
- Population trauma-exposure prevalence (% with ACE score 4+, % with adult trauma exposure)
- Social-support density (average number of close confidants per person)
- Treatment wait time (median days from referral to first appointment)
- Mental-health stigma index (survey-based)

**Evidence**: CLM-037 (community-based mental health), CLM-009 (ACEs), CLM-016 (trauma-informed design). Sources: SRC-018, SRC-019, SRC-049.

### VAL-013: Self-Sufficiency and Chosen Interdependence

**Formal definition**: Individuals, families, communities, and larger systems should have the capability to function independently for defined periods (households: weeks; communities: months; regions: 6-12 months; nation: 12-24 months). Above this floor, voluntary interdependence through trade, cooperation, and specialization is encouraged. Self-sufficiency is a floor, not a ceiling; a resilience requirement, not an isolationist ideology.

**Key indicators**:
- Household-preparedness index (% of households able to meet basic needs for 2 weeks without external supply)
- Community-reserve duration (months of essential goods available from local production + reserves)
- Local-production floor (% of calories, energy, water, and essential goods produced within N radius)
- Import-dependency concentration (Herfindahl index of critical-import sources)

**Evidence**: CLM-030 (community resilience), CLM-024 (local food production -- theoretical). Sources: SRC-002, SRC-027.

---

### VAL-014: Sustainable Progress Beyond GDP

**Formal definition**: Growth and progress must be redefined as measurable improvement in human wellbeing, ecological health, institutional quality, justice, capability, and resilience. GDP may be one indicator among many but must never be the primary metric by which societal success is judged. Metrics must capture what we value, not value what we can easily measure.

**Key indicators**:
- Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI) or equivalent composite
- Multidimensional Wellbeing Index
- Inequality-adjusted metrics (for all health, education, and wellbeing indicators)
- Ecological-drawdown rate (resource consumption relative to regeneration)
- Innovation-quality index (patent quality, scientific contribution, social innovation)
- Care-economy contribution (% of economic activity in care, recognized and measured)

**Evidence**: CLM-027 (GPI and beyond-GDP), CLM-044 (circular economy), CLM-048 (R&D investment). Sources: SRC-077, SRC-008, SRC-062.

---

### VAL-015: Privacy and Data Rights

**Formal definition**: Institutional transparency must never become surveillance of people. Personal data collection requires purpose limitation, informed consent, data minimization, security, auditability, and a defined right to access, correct, delete, and port personal data. Algorithmic decisions affecting people must be explainable, appealable, and auditable.

**Key indicators**:
- Data-breach rate (per 100,000 records)
- Surveillance-warrant rate (per 100,000 population)
- Consent-compliance audit score (% of data-collection compliant with consent requirements)
- Algorithmic-audit coverage (% of high-impact algorithms independently audited)
- Member access to personal data records (% of requests fulfilled within legal timeframe)

**Evidence**: CLM-032 (privacy-protective digital ID -- theoretical), CLM-018 (algorithmic bias). Sources: SRC-059, SRC-100, SRC-025.

---

### VAL-016: Intergenerational Stewardship

**Formal definition**: Decisions today must not deplete the resources, institutions, ecological base, fiscal capacity, or option space available to future generations. Long-term obligations must be funded, not deferred. Infrastructure must be maintained, not depreciated. Ecological capital must be preserved, not exhausted. Future people have rights that present people must respect in institutional design.

**Key indicators**:
- Long-term debt/GPI ratio (distinguishing investment from consumption)
- Ecological-debt index (cumulative resource overconsumption relative to planetary boundaries)
- Infrastructure-maintenance backlog (% of infrastructure beyond design life without renovation/replacement)
- Youth-wellbeing index (relative to overall population)
- Future-risk exposure (assessed through long-term scenario analysis -- climate, technology, fiscal, ecological)

**Evidence**: CLM-005 (planetary boundaries), CLM-016 (intergenerational stewardship as the binding constraint on present action). Sources: SRC-016, SRC-020, SRC-021.

---

### VAL-017: Care and Social Reproduction

**Formal definition**: The work of caring for children, elderly, disabled, ill, and community members is core infrastructure, not private indulgence. This includes childcare, eldercare, disability support, healthcare, mental healthcare, family leave, community care, and the maintenance of social bonds. Care work must be recognized in economic accounts, supported by public infrastructure, compensated fairly, and distributed equitably across gender.

**Key indicators**:
- Care-access index (% with access to childcare, eldercare, disability support when needed)
- Caregiver-burden score (unpaid care hours per week, by gender)
- Child-welfare index (composite of health, education, safety, development)
- Elder-isolation rate (% elderly with <1 social contact per week)
- Disability-inclusion index (access to public spaces, employment, community participation)

**Evidence**: CLM-009 (ACEs), CLM-006 (scarcity effects), CLM-041 (housing security). Sources: SRC-019, SRC-012, SRC-018.

---

### VAL-018: Continuous Self-Correction

**Formal definition**: No constitutional design is perfect. The society must therefore contain multiple, independent feedback loops: institutional self-assessment, independent audits, adversarial review, citizen oversight, legal challenges, peaceful protest, investigative journalism, academic critique, and formal amendment procedures. The capacity to recognize and correct errors is more important for long-term survival than the correctness of any founding document.

**Key indicators**:
- Amendment throughput (successful amendments per decade)
- Contradiction-resolution time (mean time from public documentation of contradiction to institutional acknowledgment)
- Audit-response rate (% of audit findings acted upon within X months)
- Reform-participation rate (% of population engaging in reform processes)
- Error-correction rate (detected material errors corrected vs. uncorrected after 1 year)

**Evidence**: CLM-025 (peaceful transfer), CLM-039 (feedback loops -- theoretical). Sources: SRC-029, SRC-010, SRC-056.

---

### VAL-019: Internal Observability and Secure Boundaries

**Formal definition**: Governance processes, public budgets, institutional performance, enforcement actions, societal-health metrics, and material-risk indicators must be observable by members in near-real-time, while individual privacy is protected and external interfaces are explicitly bounded and audited for security. This is the operationalization of "transparency for power, privacy for people, security at boundaries."

**Key indicators**:
- Public-audit coverage (% of government decisions, budgets, and enforcement actions publicly logged within X days)
- Decision-log completeness (audit of whether all material decisions have accessible records)
- Boundary-breach rate (unauthorized access across physical, cyber, data, biological, and informational external boundaries per year)
- Observability latency (median time between event and member-visible record)
- Access-control audit score (% of boundary-security controls passing independent audit)
- Member observability access rate (% of population with verified ability to access public-process observability data)

**Design constraints**:
- Government and institutional processes must default to observable.
- Exceptions require specific justification (privacy, security, investigation integrity) with time-limited suppression and mandatory post-hoc review.
- Boundary design must explicitly define what crosses each interface and under what conditions.
- Observability infrastructure must itself be observable and auditable.

**Evidence**: CLM-031 (transparency works with enforcement), CLM-032 (privacy-protective identity -- theoretical). Sources: SRC-009, SRC-034, SRC-100.

## Section 2: Value Conflict Map

This living map identifies known value conflicts. Some are resolvable through design; some are irreducible tensions that must be managed rather than eliminated.

| Conflict ID | Values in Tension | Nature of Conflict | Mitigation Approach | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| CON-VAL-001 | VAL-001 (rights) vs. VAL-004 (resilience) | Rights protections can reduce crisis-response speed and flexibility | Pre-defined emergency powers with strict limits, sunset, and post-hoc review. Rights are non-derogable; response mechanisms, not rights, must flex. | Managed, not resolved |
| CON-VAL-002 | VAL-001 (rights) vs. VAL-019 (observability) | Individual privacy vs. institutional transparency | "Transparency for power, privacy for people" as design principle. Anonymized aggregated metrics. Strict separation of institutional and personal data streams. | Managed |
| CON-VAL-003 | VAL-003 (ecological boundaries) vs. VAL-004 (resilience) | Ecological constraints may limit redundancy and backup options | Redundancy must be designed within ecological boundaries, not by exceeding them. | CON-VAL-004 | VAL-005 (consent governance) vs. VAL-006 (capture resistance) | Democratic responsiveness to majority may enable capture of minority protections | Supermajority requirements for rights changes. Independent rights-protection bodies not subject to electoral pressure. Constitutional courts with appointments insulated from electoral cycles. | Structural mitigation |
| CON-VAL-005 | VAL-010 (pluralism) vs. VAL-011 (education) | Diverse cultural approaches to education may conflict with shared civic-competence standards | Core civic-competence standards are non-negotiable and apply universally. Cultural curricula are autonomous within those bounds. Thin standards, thick autonomy. | Managed |
| CON-VAL-006 | VAL-013 (self-sufficiency) vs. VAL-014 (beyond-GDP progress) | Self-sufficiency investments may reduce short-term efficiency and economic growth | Self-sufficiency is a resilience floor, not an efficiency ceiling. The boundary between public and private must be explicitly defined and adjudicated. See VAL-019 design constraints. | Ongoing design task |

## Section 3: Conflict Resolution Rules

When values compete in a specific design decision, the agent (or governance body in a living society) must follow this procedure:

1. **Identify**: List all affected value IDs (e.g., VAL-004 vs. VAL-003).
2. **Describe**: Articulate the conflict in concrete institutional terms. Avoid abstractions. "Building redundant grid infrastructure requires land-use that may violate ecological boundaries" not "resilience vs. ecology."
3. **Propose options**: Generate at least two resolution options, each respecting different value weightings.
4. **Cost allocation**: State explicitly who bears the cost of each option (which population, which generation, which region).
5. **Decision criteria**: Define what evidence or simulation result would favor each option. "If simulation shows a 30%+ reduction in crisis recovery time for the redundancy option, and ecological-impact assessment shows boundary transgression below X threshold, option A is favored."
6. **Record**: All resolution decisions must be recorded in the decision log with full rationale, alternatives considered, and revisit triggers.

Values are not rank-ordered in this constitution. Each conflict is resolved contextually through this procedure. No value may be permanently subordinated to another.

## Section 4: Amendment Rules

Any value may be amended, but amendments require:

1. **Proposal**: A named change proposal with stable ID (e.g., AMEND-001).
2. **Rationale**: Grounded in evidence, contradiction resolution, simulation results, or demonstrated failure of current formulation.
3. **Compatibility analysis**: Impact assessment on all 19 other values.
4. **Adversarial review**: From skeptic, historian, and behavioral-scientist perspectives.
5. **Migration plan**: List of affected artifacts and what would change.
6. **Ratification**: Supermajority threshold (65%+) in both deliberative assembly and citizen referendum.
7. **Implementation delay**: Minimum 1-year cooling-off period between ratification and effect.

## Section 5: Value Inheritance
