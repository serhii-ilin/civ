# ART-015: Ecology and Infrastructure System Architecture

Iteration: `baseline-v0.1`
Status: draft

## Purpose

Design the ecological, spatial, and infrastructure foundations for a durable society that operates within planetary boundaries while providing resilience, wellbeing, and prosperity.

## Core Design Principles

1. **Planetary boundaries are hard constraints**: All economic, industrial, and social activity must operate within Rockström/Steffen planetary boundaries (CLM-005).
2. **Local production floors with global trade ceilings**: Essential goods must be producible locally to defined resilience levels. Above the floor, comparative advantage and trade are encouraged.
3. **Circular, not linear**: Materials are designed to stay in use. Waste is designed out. Regeneration is designed in (CLM-044).
4. **Resilience through redundancy and modularity**: Critical infrastructure has n+1 redundancy. Systems are modular, enabling graceful degradation.
5. **Spatial design for health and community**: The built environment shapes health, social connection, and ecological impact (CLM-023, CLM-041).
6. **Land is a commons with private use rights**: Land value created by community and nature belongs to the community (Georgist principle). Private use rights are protected within ecological and community constraints.

## Ecological Architecture

### Planetary Boundaries Compliance

The society commits to operating within all nine planetary boundaries at per-capita allocation consistent with global equity:

| Boundary | Current Global Status | Target (Per Capita, Illustrative) | Policy Mechanism |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Climate change (CO2) | Transgressed (high risk) | Carbon budget aligned with 1.5C pathway; net-zero by 2040-2050 | Carbon price + border adjustment + renewable mandate + efficiency standards |
| Biosphere integrity | Transgressed (high risk) | Net-zero biodiversity loss; 30%+ of land/water protected | Land-use regulation; biodiversity offsets; ecological restoration mandate |
| Land-system change | Transgressed (increasing risk) | No net forest loss; reforestation target | Land-use planning; agricultural intensification on existing land; forest protection |
| Freshwater use | Approaching boundary | Sustainable withdrawal within renewable supply per watershed | Water pricing; efficiency standards; watershed governance |
| Biogeochemical flows (N, P) | Transgressed (high risk) | Nutrient budgets for agriculture; closed-loop nutrient cycling | Agricultural regulation; wastewater treatment; circular nutrient economy |
| Ocean acidification | Approaching boundary | Addressed through CO2 boundary compliance | Carbon price drives emissions reduction |
| Atmospheric aerosol loading | Regional transgression | Air-quality standards meeting WHO guidelines | Emissions standards; industrial regulation; transport electrification |
| Novel entities (chemical pollution) | Transgressed | Precautionary principle; chemical-by-chemical phaseout of persistent, bioaccumulative toxics | Chemical regulation; extended producer responsibility |
| Stratospheric ozone depletion | Within boundary (recovering) | Maintain Montreal Protocol compliance | International treaty compliance |

**Decision**: DEC-ECOINF-001 (planetary-boundaries compliance framework). Rationale: Planetary boundaries framework is the best available science on safe operating space for humanity (CLM-005, SRC-020, SRC-021). Framework uncertainty on specific boundary values is acknowledged but precautionary approach is warranted given irreversibility of many transgressions.

### Ecological Accounting

- **Natural capital accounts**: National balance sheet includes natural capital (forests, water, soil, biodiversity, carbon sinks). Depletion is measured against regeneration.
- **Ecological budget**: Government budgeting includes ecological budget alongside fiscal budget. Deficit spending on ecology is as concerning as fiscal deficit.
- **Environmental impact assessment**: All major policies and projects require environmental-impact assessment with independent review. Assessments must consider climate, biodiversity, water, land, pollution, and cumulative effects.
- **Ecological Guardian**: Independent body (parallel to central bank independence) with mandate to assess ecological compliance, publish findings, and refer violations for enforcement. Director appointed for 10-year non-renewable term; budget indexed.

### Climate Adaptation
- **Infrastructure hardening**: Upgraded for higher temperatures, extreme weather, sea-level rise.
- **Managed retreat**: Planned relocation from high-risk coastal and fire-prone areas, with community consent and support.
- **Agricultural adaptation**: Crop diversification, drought-resistant varieties, water-efficient irrigation, agroforestry.
- **Ecosystem adaptation**: Wildlife corridors, assisted migration where necessary, ecosystem restoration.
- **Heat resilience**: Urban greening, cool roofs, cooling centers, community heat plans.
- **Flood resilience**: Natural flood management (wetland restoration, floodplain reconnection), hard defenses where necessary, flood-resistant building codes.

## Food System Architecture

### Production Model
**Regenerative agriculture** as default: practices that build soil health, sequester carbon, enhance biodiversity, and improve water retention. Includes: agroecology, agroforestry, silvopasture, rotational grazing, cover cropping, no-till, integrated pest management.

### Food Sovereignty by Scale

| Scale | Production Requirement | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Household | Capacity for basic food production (garden, balcony, community plot). Not mandatory but supported and normative. | VAL-013; resilience floor |
| Community | Community gardens, orchards, food forests providing significant portion of fresh produce. Community composting and food-waste recycling. | 3-month community resilience; CLM-030 on community recovery |
| Regional | Regional food system producing 50%+ of regional caloric needs. Diversified crops and livestock. Regional processing, storage, and distribution. Farmer cooperatives. | 6-month regional resilience; food-system localization reduces transport emissions and supply-chain risk |
| National | National food security reserve of 12 months of staple calories. Strategic grain reserves. Seed banks. Fertilizer production capacity. | 12-month national resilience; RSK-003 ecological overshoot risk |

### Food System Features
- **Farmer cooperatives and land trusts**: Agricultural land held in community land trusts or cooperative ownership. Farmers have secure tenure. Farmland protected from development.
- **Fair prices**: Price floors for farmers producing ecologically. Price controls on staples during crisis. Eliminate the "cheap food" policy that externalizes ecological and health costs.
- **Reduced food waste**: Target 50%+ reduction from baseline. Food-waste hierarchy: prevention > donation > animal feed > composting > energy recovery.
- **Dietary transition**: Policies to shift toward plant-rich diets (health and ecological benefits). Information, not mandate. Public procurement leads (schools, hospitals, government cafeterias).
- **Urban agriculture**: Rooftop gardens, vertical farming (energy-efficient), community gardens, edible landscaping. Supplements rural production; does not replace it.

## Water System Architecture

### Watershed Governance
- **Watershed as management unit**: Water governed by natural watershed boundaries, not political boundaries. Watershed councils with stakeholder representation (farmers, cities, ecosystems, indigenous communities, industry).
- **Water rights**: Water is public trust. Extraction rights are permits, not property. Permits tradable within sustainability caps. Basic human water right (50-100 liters/person/day) is non-tradable and guaranteed free.
- **Ecosystem water reserve**: Minimum ecological flows legally protected. Cannot be traded or extracted.

### Water Infrastructure
- **Source protection**: Watershed protection through land conservation, riparian buffers, wetland preservation.
- **Diverse sources**: Surface water, groundwater (sustainably managed), rainwater harvesting, water recycling, desalination (renewable-powered, last resort).
- **Water efficiency**: Universal metering with progressive pricing (basic allowance free). Leak-detection and repair programs. Water-efficient appliances and fixtures mandated. Drought-tolerant landscaping.
- **Green infrastructure**: Rain gardens, permeable pavement, green roofs, bioswales -- manage stormwater naturally while creating amenity and habitat.
- **Sanitation**: Wastewater treatment to tertiary standard. Nutrient recovery (phosphorus recycling). Water recycling for non-potable uses. Decentralized treatment where appropriate.

### Flood and Drought Resilience
- **Natural flood management**: Wetland restoration, floodplain reconnection, river restoration, upland tree planting.
- **Sponge-city design**: Urban design that absorbs and stores rainwater rather than channeling it away.
- **Drought preparedness**: Water storage (reservoirs, aquifers, tanks), drought-resistant landscaping, water-sharing agreements, drought-emergency protocols with equitable rationing.

## Energy System Architecture

### 100% Renewable Target
- **Timeline**: Net-zero emissions electricity by 2035; net-zero energy system by 2050.
- **Mix**: Solar (rooftop + utility), wind (onshore + offshore), geothermal, hydro (existing + small run-of-river), sustainable biomass (limited, for hard-to-electrify sectors), energy storage (battery, pumped hydro, green hydrogen, thermal).
- **No new fossil-fuel infrastructure**: No new coal, oil, or gas extraction, power plants, or pipelines. Existing phased out on declining schedule with just-transition support for workers and communities.
- **No nuclear new-build in baseline** (this is a design decision, not anti-nuclear ideology -- alternative iteration could explore nuclear pathway; this baseline prioritizes renewables + storage given cost trajectories, waste governance challenges, and proliferation risk).

### Grid Architecture
- **Smart, bidirectional grid**: Enables distributed generation, demand response, vehicle-to-grid.
- **Community microgrids**: Every community cell has islanding capability (can disconnect and operate independently for resilience).
- **Regional interconnection**: Regions interconnected for load balancing and resilience, but with firebreaks to prevent cascading failure (RSK-016).
- **Storage**: Distributed battery storage at household, community, and grid scale. Pumped hydro where geography permits.

### Energy Democracy
- **Community energy cooperatives**: Preferred ownership model for renewable generation. Communities own their energy production where feasible.
- **Public utility model**: Grid operated as public or cooperative utility, not for-profit monopoly.
- **Energy as commons**: Basic energy allowance (sufficient for heating/cooling, cooking, lighting, and essential devices) provided universally. Progressive pricing above allowance.

## Housing and Land-Use Architecture

### Universal Housing Guarantee
- Every person has right to safe, adequate, affordable housing (CLM-041).
- Delivery models: social housing (public), cooperative housing, community land trusts, regulated private rental, private ownership.
- No one sleeps rough. Housing First model: provide housing without preconditions, then wrap support services.

### Land Governance
- **Land Value Tax (LVT)** (MOD-ECO): Primary land-taxation mechanism. Captures socially created land value for public benefit. Discourages speculation and vacant land holding.
- **Community Land Trusts**: Non-profit entities holding land in trust for community benefit. Land removed from speculative market. Housing on CLT land is permanently affordable. Preferred model for new development.
- **Land-use planning**: Democratic, participatory, integrated. Regional plans set framework; communities make local decisions within framework.
- **Greenbelts and growth boundaries**: Contain sprawl, protect agricultural land and ecosystems. Combined with sufficient density to meet housing need.
- **Land-value capture**: Infrastructure investments that increase land values trigger automatic value-capture mechanism (tax increment financing or equivalent) to fund the infrastructure.

### Spatial and Urban Design
- **15-minute neighborhood**: Essential services, work, education, recreation within 15-minute walk or bike. Per CLM-023 evidence on walkability and health.
- **Transit-oriented development**: Density around public-transport nodes. Reduced car dependency. Parking maximums, not minimums.
- **Mixed-use zoning**: Residential, commercial, light industrial, and civic uses integrated, not separated. Eliminate single-use zoning.
- **Green space minimums**: Every resident within 5-minute walk of significant green space (>0.5 hectare). Urban tree-canopy targets (30%+).
- **Affordable housing inclusion**: All new developments above threshold size include affordable units (20-30%). Or equivalent in-lieu fee to public housing fund.
- **Adaptive reuse and preservation**: Prioritize renovation and adaptation of existing buildings over demolition. Embodied carbon in existing buildings is an asset.

### Mobility
- **Hierarchy**: Walking > Cycling > Public Transport > Shared Vehicles > Private Vehicles
- **Public transport**: Frequent, reliable, affordable, accessible, electric. Free at point of use in urban areas.
- **Active transport**: Comprehensive network of protected bike lanes and pedestrian paths. Bike-sharing systems. End-of-trip facilities.
- **Electric vehicles**: Transition to electric for remaining private vehicles. Charging infrastructure ubiquitous. Right-to-repair and battery-recycling requirements.
- **Car reduction**: Reduced car dependency through urban design, not prohibition. Car-free zones in dense urban cores. Congestion pricing.

## Circular Economy and Materials

### Principles
- **Design out waste**: Products designed for durability, repairability, modularity, and eventual disassembly.
- **Keep materials in use**: Reuse, repair, remanufacture, recycle. Highest-value use preserved at each cycle.
- **Regenerate natural systems**: Biological materials returned to soil. Extraction minimized.
- **Producer responsibility**: Producers responsible for entire product lifecycle (Extended Producer Responsibility -- EPR).

### Implementation Mechanisms
- **Right to repair**: Mandatory. Manufacturers must provide parts, tools, manuals, and software access for repair. Anti-tampering and parts-pairing restrictions prohibited.
- **Material passports**: Building and major products tracked with material composition for future recycling. Digital product passports.
- **Deposit-return systems**: Universal for beverage containers, batteries, electronics. High deposit values drive return.
- **Landfill and incineration taxes**: Rising over time. Near-zero waste-to-landfill target.
- **Repair cafes, tool libraries, sharing economy**: Community-level infrastructure for circular economy. Makerspaces with tools and expertise.
- **Public procurement**: Circular-economy criteria in all public purchasing.

### Industrial Symbiosis
- Industrial parks designed for resource exchange: one facility's waste is another's input.
- Heat networks: industrial waste heat used for district heating.
- Shared infrastructure: water treatment, logistics, energy.

## Infrastructure Resilience Standards

### Critical Infrastructure Classification
- **Category A (Existential)**: Failure could threaten survival of population or civilization. Grid, water, food system, healthcare, communications. n+2 redundancy. Manual fallback for all digital systems. 24-month disruption survival.
- **Category B (Critical)**: Failure would cause severe hardship. Transport, sanitation, education, finance. n+1 redundancy. 6-month disruption survival.
- **Category C (Important)**: Failure would reduce quality of life. Recreation, cultural venues, non-essential services. Standard resilience.

### Resilience Implementation
- **Distributed generation**: No single-point-of-failure infrastructure. Distributed energy, water treatment, food production, data storage.
- **Secure supply chains**: Strategic reserves of critical materials, medicines, and components. Domestic production capacity for essentials.
- **Cyber-physical separation**: Critical infrastructure control systems air-gapped or securely isolated from public internet.
- **Manual fallback**: Every critical digital system has documented, tested, manual-operation procedure.
- **Regular stress testing**: Annual resilience exercises. Red-team attacks on infrastructure. Cascading-failure modeling (RSK-016).
